India's Balancing Act: Russia, US, and the Art of Strategic Autonomy

NV
Nikhil Varma Geopolitics Lead
·10 min read·May 14, 2026
⚖️

India buys discounted crude from Russia by the tanker-load. It also runs joint military exercises with the United States, buys American fighter jet engines, and shares intelligence through the Quad. In most capitals, doing both at once would trigger a diplomatic crisis. In New Delhi, it's Tuesday.

The doctrine behind the balancing

India calls this "strategic autonomy" — the idea that it will make foreign policy decisions purely on the basis of national interest, refusing permanent alignment with any single power bloc. It's a direct descendant of Cold War-era non-alignment, updated for a world where India actually has leverage to use, rather than just principles to defend.

"Every other major power treats India's refusal to choose sides as a problem to be managed. India treats it as the entire point." — Nikhil Varma

How the balance actually gets struck

With Russia, the relationship is anchored in decades of defense dependency — a large share of India's military hardware, from fighter jets to submarines, is still of Russian origin, and spare parts and maintenance contracts lock in the relationship for years. Discounted oil imports since 2022 have been a economic bonus layered on top of a security relationship India isn't eager to unwind quickly.

With the United States, the relationship is newer but faster-growing — built on shared concerns about China, deepening technology transfer agreements, and a large Indian-American diaspora that gives it unusual domestic political weight in Washington. The 2023 and 2024 defense technology agreements moved India closer to being treated as a genuine security partner rather than just a large arms customer.

  • Leverage point #1: India's market size makes it too big for either power to alienate over the other relationship.
  • Leverage point #2: India's position on China gives Washington a reason to tolerate the Russia ties.
  • Leverage point #3: India's economic growth gives Moscow a reason to tolerate the US ties.

Where the tightrope gets thinner

The Ukraine war has been the sharpest stress test yet. India has abstained from most UN votes condemning Russia, drawing criticism from Western partners, while simultaneously increasing defense cooperation with the US and Europe. Each side has, so far, decided that a partially-aligned India is better than a fully-alienated one. But that patience isn't infinite — the more the Quad hardens into a formal security bloc, and the more sanctions regimes tighten around Russia, the smaller India's room to maneuver becomes.

For now, strategic autonomy is working precisely because India has enough weight to make both Washington and Moscow adjust to it, rather than the other way around. That's a rare position for any middle power to hold — and an increasingly hard one to hold onto as the room for straddling both camps keeps shrinking.
