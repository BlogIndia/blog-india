The Indus Waters Treaty Is Cracking, and Nobody Agrees on Who Broke It

NV
Nikhil Varma Geopolitics Lead
·10 min read·July 6, 2026
💧

The Indus Waters Treaty survived two wars, a nuclear standoff, and six decades of otherwise near-total diplomatic breakdown between India and Pakistan. It is, by most accounts, one of the most durable water-sharing agreements between hostile states anywhere in the world. Which is what makes its current status so unusual: India considers it in abeyance since April 2025, following the terror attack in Pahalgam, while Pakistan insists it remains "fully operational and effective." Two countries, reading the same 1960 treaty, arriving at opposite conclusions about whether it still exists.

A dispute that keeps generating new disputes

The friction isn't staying abstract. Pakistan's Indus water commissioner has reportedly written to his Indian counterpart multiple times — twice in 2025 and again in May 2026 — over what Islamabad describes as abnormal, abrupt flow variations on the Chenab river. Each letter is essentially the same argument restated: that India, by treating the treaty as suspended, is making unilateral decisions about shared river flows that used to require joint oversight. On January 30, 2026, Pakistan's Permanent Representative to the United Nations went further, telling the UN that India's position constitutes a serious violation of international legal obligations, with what Islamabad frames as humanitarian, environmental, and security consequences.

A treaty built to keep water out of the war column has instead become one of the more closely watched pressure points on the subcontinent — precisely because both sides know how much leverage sits behind it.

Why the eastern-western river split matters

To understand the current standoff, it helps to remember what the treaty actually did in 1960: it split the Indus system's six rivers, giving India exclusive use of the eastern rivers — the Sutlej, Beas, and Ravi — while granting Pakistan primary rights to the western rivers, the Indus, Jhelum, and Chenab, with India retaining limited, non-consumptive uses like hydropower on those western flows. India's Bhakra Dam on the Sutlej, along with the Pong and Pandoh dams on the Beas and the Ranjit Sagar dam on the Ravi, are part of the infrastructure that let India put roughly 95% of its eastern river allocation to use over the decades. Pakistan's current objections are aimed less at that long-settled eastern allocation and more at India's newer hydropower projects on the western rivers — the ones where India's rights were always narrower, and where "abeyance" now gives India far more room to maneuver without the treaty's dispute-resolution mechanisms constraining it.

  • India's position: the treaty is in abeyance, a direct consequence of the April 2025 Pahalgam attack and the security calculus that followed.
  • Pakistan's position: the treaty has no abeyance clause, remains legally binding, and India's western-river hydropower activity now lacks the oversight the treaty was designed to guarantee.
  • The pattern: this is the third major dispute raised under the treaty framework in roughly eighteen months — a frequency with no real precedent in the agreement's 65-year history.

What actually happens if the water becomes a weapon

The nightmare scenario often invoked in commentary — India simply "turning off" the rivers that feed Pakistani agriculture — is more complicated in practice than in rhetoric. India lacks the storage infrastructure on the western rivers to meaningfully withhold their flow at scale; the leverage today looks more like reduced predictability and reduced information-sharing than an actual dam-controlled shutoff. But predictability is exactly what large-scale irrigation agriculture depends on, and Pakistan's Punjab breadbasket runs on precisely that predictability. A slow erosion of data-sharing and joint oversight can do real damage to downstream planning long before any dam gate closes.

That's the real story here, and it's a quieter one than "water war" headlines suggest: not a single dramatic rupture, but a treaty's institutional machinery — the joint commission meetings, the data exchanges, the dispute mechanisms — being allowed to go dormant while both governments keep arguing about whose interpretation of a 1960 document is correct. Treaties rarely die in a single dramatic moment. More often, as the Indus Waters Treaty may now be demonstrating, they die from eighteen months of unanswered letters.
