The Ayurveda Revival: Ancient Wisdom Meets Modern Wellness

RS
Dr. Rohan Seth Health Editor
·9 min read·June 12, 2026
🌿

Walk into a wellness store in Berlin, Los Angeles, or Singapore today and you'll find ashwagandha capsules, turmeric lattes, and "dosha-balancing" skincare lines sitting next to the multivitamins. Ayurveda — India's 5,000-year-old system of medicine — has quietly become one of the most exported ideas in global wellness. The question worth asking is: how much of it actually holds up?

What Ayurveda gets right

Modern research has validated several Ayurvedic staples. Turmeric's active compound, curcumin, has documented anti-inflammatory properties, though absorption issues mean most supplements need piperine or fat pairing to be effective. Ashwagandha has reasonable trial evidence for reducing cortisol and improving sleep quality. Triphala, a three-fruit blend used for centuries as a digestive aid, has small but consistent studies supporting its use for mild constipation.

More broadly, Ayurveda's core philosophy — that diet, sleep, movement, and mental state are inseparable from physical health — anticipated by millennia what modern medicine now calls "lifestyle medicine." That framing, more than any single herb, may be its most valuable export.

"The West didn't discover holistic health. It rediscovered it, and gave it a marketing budget." — Dr. Rohan Seth

Where the evidence runs thin

Not everything survives contact with a randomized controlled trial. Claims around "detoxification" panchakarma treatments, dosha-based personality typing, and some heavy-metal-containing traditional formulations (bhasmas) have either failed to show benefit or raised genuine safety concerns — a handful of documented lead and mercury poisoning cases have been traced to poorly regulated Ayurvedic products, mostly manufactured outside India's stricter pharmacopeia standards.

  • Reasonably well-supported: turmeric/curcumin, ashwagandha for stress, triphala for digestion.
  • Mixed or preliminary evidence: brahmi for cognition, neem for skin conditions.
  • Caution warranted: unregulated heavy-metal formulations, aggressive "detox" claims.

The path forward

India's Ministry of AYUSH has been pushing for more rigorous clinical trials of traditional formulations, and a few Indian pharmaceutical companies now run Ayurvedic products through the same trial pipelines as conventional drugs. That's the right instinct: Ayurveda doesn't need to choose between tradition and evidence. The compounds that work will keep working under a microscope. The ones that don't deserve to be retired, however old they are.
