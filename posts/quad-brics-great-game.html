Quad, BRICS, and the New Great Game: Where India Stands

NV
Nikhil Varma Geopolitics Lead
·11 min read·June 5, 2026
🧭

On paper, it shouldn't work. India sits in the Quad — alongside the US, Japan, and Australia — a grouping widely understood as a soft counterweight to China. It also sits in BRICS, alongside China and Russia, a bloc explicitly built to challenge Western-dominated financial institutions. Most countries are asked to pick a side. India has, so far, simply declined the question.

Two tables, one seat each

The Quad gives India defense cooperation, technology-sharing agreements, and a hedge against Chinese assertiveness in the Indian Ocean. BRICS gives India a voice in reshaping global financial governance — a New Development Bank that doesn't route every loan through Washington, and a forum where "Global South" concerns about IMF conditionality actually get airtime. These aren't contradictory goals. They're two different insurance policies against two different risks: military encirclement on one side, financial marginalization on the other.

"Non-alignment used to mean staying out of both camps. Strategic autonomy means having a seat in every camp that matters." — Nikhil Varma

The friction points

This balancing act isn't free. India's Quad partners have periodically pushed back on its continued defense purchases from Russia and its reluctance to publicly condemn Moscow over Ukraine with the same language the US and Europe use. Meanwhile, China's dominant role in BRICS — and its border standoff with India in Ladakh — means India often finds itself negotiating bloc positions with its most direct strategic rival sitting across the table.

  • With the Quad: deepening defense-tech transfer, but resisting formal alliance-style commitments.
  • With BRICS: pushing de-dollarization initiatives for trade convenience, while stopping short of anything that reads as anti-US.
  • The constant: India votes for whatever specific proposal serves its interests that year, not for bloc loyalty.

Why it's working, for now

India's leverage comes from scale — a market and manufacturing base too large for either camp to ignore — and from timing. Both the US and China currently have more urgent priorities than forcing New Delhi's hand. That won't last forever. As the Quad hardens into something closer to a formal security architecture, and as BRICS expansion brings in more explicitly anti-Western members, India's room to sit at both tables will narrow. The interesting geopolitical question of the next five years isn't whether India picks a side — it's how long it can keep not picking one.
